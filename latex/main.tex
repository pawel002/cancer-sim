\begin{abstract}
Predictive modeling of cancer therapy operates under high uncertainty, limited
patient-specific data, and strong computational constraints.  High-fidelity PDE
models are difficult to calibrate in this regime, while purely data-driven models
remain sensitive to noise and data scarcity.  We propose a two-stage surrogate for
tumour dynamics under radiotherapy.  In the first stage a spatially-averaged ODE
provides a fast, physically consistent baseline.  In the second stage a compact
multi-layer perceptron (MLP), trained offline on a library of PDE simulations,
corrects the structural residual arising from spatial heterogeneity.  The corrector
receives a ten-dimensional feature vector that encodes the spatial configuration of
the tumour and the radiation beam without requiring a spatial grid at inference.
Evaluated on a cross-product of four tumour initial-condition distributions and four
beam distributions, the ODE+MLP surrogate matches PDE accuracy across all sixteen
scenarios while remaining between one and two orders of magnitude faster.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Optimizing radiotherapy treatment planning remains a fundamental challenge in
computational oncology.  Reliable prediction of tumour response requires models
capable of capturing the interplay between growth, spatial heterogeneity, and
treatment effects under complex irradiation protocols.  Mechanistic,
spatio-temporal models based on reaction-diffusion partial differential equations
provide a principled description of these processes
\cite{murray2002mathematical,swanson2000quantitative,tracqui2009biophysical}, but
their practical use is severely limited.  The main difficulty lies in the regime in
which therapy prediction operates: high uncertainty, limited patient-specific data,
and strong computational constraints \cite{yankeelov2013clinically,hormuth2021mechanically}.
Increasing model complexity in this setting amplifies parameter non-identifiability,
sensitivity to noise, and computational cost, making high-fidelity PDE models
unreliable for patient-specific forecasting \cite{altrock2015mathematics,oden2016toward}.

Purely data-driven approaches face complementary limitations.  In realistic clinical
scenarios available data are scarce, heterogeneous, and collected under changing
conditions.  The absence of physical constraints may lead to non-physical or unstable
predictions \cite{karniadakis2021physics,brunton2022data}.

These observations motivate a hybrid strategy: reduce dimensionality while preserving
the dominant physics, and supplement the reduced model with a compact data-driven
correction that targets its specific structural failures
\cite{raissi2019physics,reichstein2019deep}.  The main contributions of
this work are as follows.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Reduction-oriented ODE baseline.}  We derive a spatially-averaged
    macroscopic ODE from the reaction-diffusion PDE and characterise three distinct
    sources of error: spatial non-linearity, inconsistent radiation response, and
    missing biology.

    \item \textbf{Spatial feature engineering.}  We show that the dominant closure
    error (the inconsistent radiation response) can be summarised by a small set of
    scenario-level scalars (tumour and beam centres of mass, their distance, effective
    hypoxia, effective beam amplitude) computed once from initial fields without a
    spatial grid.

    \item \textbf{Offline MLP residual corrector.}  A compact MLP trained on a
    library of randomised PDE simulations learns to predict the residual
    $\delta(t) = U_{\mathrm{PDE}}(t) - U_{\mathrm{ODE}}(t)$ from these spatial
    features plus the current ODE prediction.  The corrected surrogate achieves
    near-PDE accuracy across all tested spatial configurations at inference cost
    indistinguishable from the plain ODE.
\end{itemize}

\section{Tumour under Therapy}

\subsection{The Spatio-Temporal Model}

The spatio-temporal dynamics of tumour growth and response to therapy are commonly
described using reaction-diffusion equations \cite{murray2002mathematical,cristini2010multiscale}.
A widely used formulation combines cell proliferation, spatial invasion, and
treatment effects \cite{swanson2000quantitative,jarrett2020optimal}:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:pde}
    \frac{\partial u(x, t)}{\partial t} = D \nabla^2 u
        + \rho u \!\left(1 - \frac{u}{K}\right)
        - \beta R(x, t) H(x) u
        + \zeta(u, x, t),
\end{equation}

where $u(x,t)$ is the local tumour cell density, $D$ is the diffusion coefficient,
$\rho$ is the proliferation rate, $K$ is the carrying capacity, $\beta$ is the
radiosensitivity \cite{brenner2008linear,hall2006radiobiology}, $R(x,t)$ is the
local dose distribution, $H(x)$ accounts for spatial variations in radiosensitivity
due to hypoxia \cite{vaupel2004hypoxia,tannock1972relation}, and $\zeta$ represents
unresolved biology.

\subsection{Derivation of the Macroscopic Surrogate}

Clinical measurements capture the macroscopic tumour mass
$y(t) = \int_\Omega u(x,t)\,\mathrm{d}x$.
Differentiating and substituting~\eqref{eq:pde}, and noting that the diffusion term
vanishes under no-flux boundary conditions, yields the exact dynamics:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:exact_macroscopic}
    \dot{y}(t) = \rho y(t)
        - \frac{\rho}{K} \!\int_\Omega u^2\,\mathrm{d}x
        - \beta \!\int_\Omega R(x,t)H(x)u\,\mathrm{d}x
        + \!\int_\Omega \zeta\,\mathrm{d}x.
\end{equation}

Replacing the integrals by mean-field approximations gives the ODE surrogate:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:ode_surrogate}
    \dot{y}(t) = \rho y(t) - \alpha y^2(t) - \beta_{\mathrm{eff}}\, c(t)\, y(t),
\end{equation}

where $\alpha = \rho/K_{\mathrm{eff}}$, $\beta_{\mathrm{eff}}$ is an effective
radiosensitivity, and $c(t)$ is the scalar therapy protocol.  Comparing
\eqref{eq:exact_macroscopic} and \eqref{eq:ode_surrogate} isolates three error
sources:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Spatial non-linearity:}
      $\tfrac{\rho}{K}\!\int u^2\,\mathrm{d}x \neq \alpha y^2$.
\item \textbf{Inconsistent radiation response:}
      $\beta\!\int R H u\,\mathrm{d}x \neq \beta_{\mathrm{eff}} c(t) y$.
\item \textbf{Missing biology:}
      $\int \zeta\,\mathrm{d}x \neq 0$.
\end{enumerate}

The second error is particularly severe when the radiation beam is spatially
misaligned with the tumour: the mean-field estimate $\beta_{\mathrm{eff}} c(t)y$
is insensitive to whether the beam actually covers the tumour mass.

\subsection{Neural Residual Corrector for the ODE Surrogate}
\label{sec:mlp}

\subsubsection{Motivation.}
Let $U(t) = y(t)/|\Omega|$ be the normalised spatial average.  Define the ODE
residual

\begin{equation}
    \delta(t;\,\theta) = U_{\mathrm{PDE}}(t;\,\theta) - U_{\mathrm{ODE}}(t;\,\theta),
\end{equation}

where $\theta$ denotes the spatial scenario parameters (tumour IC, beam profile,
tissue heterogeneity).  The residual is a structured, scenario-dependent signal
that cannot be reduced by tuning $\alpha$ or $\beta_{\mathrm{eff}}$ alone, but can
be predicted from a compact description of the spatial configuration.

\subsubsection{Feature vector.}
We summarise each scenario by a ten-dimensional vector $\mathbf{s}_t$ assembled from
the ODE trajectory and spatial statistics computed once from initial fields:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:features}
    \mathbf{s}_t = \Bigl[
        U^{\mathrm{ODE}}(t),\;
        \tfrac{t}{T},\;
        r(t),\;
        \tfrac{z^{\mathrm{ic}}_x}{L},\;
        \tfrac{z^{\mathrm{ic}}_y}{L},\;
        \tfrac{z^{\mathrm{bm}}_x}{L},\;
        \tfrac{z^{\mathrm{bm}}_y}{L},\;
        \tfrac{d}{L},\;
        \bar{H},\;
        \bar{R}
    \Bigr].
\end{equation}

Here $z^{\mathrm{ic}}$ and $z^{\mathrm{bm}}$ are the IC and beam centres of mass,
$d = \|z^{\mathrm{ic}} - z^{\mathrm{bm}}\|$ is the spatial misalignment,
$r(t) \in \{0,1\}$ is the radiation gate, $\bar{H}$ is the IC-weighted mean
hypoxia, and $\bar{R}$ is the IC-weighted beam amplitude.  All entries except
$U^{\mathrm{ODE}}(t)$ and $t$ are scenario constants.

\subsubsection{Network architecture.}
The corrector $g_\phi : \mathbb{R}^{10} \to \mathbb{R}$ is a three-hidden-layer MLP
\cite{hornik1989multilayer,cybenko1989approximation}:

\begin{equation}
    g_\phi(\mathbf{s}) = \mathbf{W}_4 \,
    \mathrm{GELU}\!\left(
      \mathbf{W}_3 \,
      \mathrm{LN}\!\bigl(\mathrm{GELU}(
        \mathbf{W}_2 \, \mathrm{LN}(
          \mathrm{GELU}(\mathbf{W}_1 \mathbf{s} + \mathbf{b}_1)
        ) + \mathbf{b}_2
      )\bigr) + \mathbf{b}_3
    \right) + b_4,
\end{equation}

with hidden dimensions $(64, 64, 32)$, GELU activations \cite{hendrycks2016gelu},
LayerNorm after the first two layers \cite{ba2016layer}, and approximately 6\,500
trainable parameters.

\subsubsection{Training.}
Parameters $\phi$ are optimised offline by minimising the mean squared residual over
$N_{\mathrm{train}}$ randomly generated scenarios:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:loss}
    \min_{\phi}\;\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{train}} T}
    \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\mathrm{train}}} \sum_{t}
    \bigl\|g_\phi(\mathbf{s}_t^{(i)}) - \delta(t;\,\theta_i)\bigr\|^2.
\end{equation}

The corrected prediction at inference is:

\begin{equation} \label{eq:corrected}
    U^{\mathrm{NC}}(t) = \max\!\bigl(U^{\mathrm{ODE}}(t) + g_\phi(\mathbf{s}_t),\; 0\bigr).
\end{equation}

No spatial grid evaluation is required during inference: the scenario scalars in
$\mathbf{s}_t$ are pre-computed from initial conditions, and $g_\phi$ is a single
forward pass over the ODE trajectory.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\subsubsection{PDE model.}
Simulations use a $50 \times 50$ finite-difference discretisation of domain
$\Omega = [0, L]^2$ with $L = 10$ (spatial units), time horizon $T = 40$ (days),
and step $\Delta t = 0.05$.  Physical parameters are $D = 0.01$, $\rho = 0.01$,
$K = 1$, $\beta = 0.7$, radiation active on $t \in [10, 30]$.  The hypoxia map
$H(x) = 0.3 + 0.7\exp(-\|x - x_c\|^2/(L/2)^2)$ is highest at the domain centre
$x_c$.

\subsubsection{Spatial distributions.}
To stress-test spatial generalisation we define four named distributions for both
the tumour initial condition and the radiation beam, independently:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Left / Right}: a single Gaussian peak at $x = L/4$ or $3L/4$.
    \item \textbf{Middle}: a single peak centred at $x = L/2$.
    \item \textbf{Two Peaks}: two co-equal peaks at $L/4$ and $3L/4$.
\end{itemize}

Combining all four IC distributions with all four beam distributions gives a
$4 \times 4 = 16$-scenario cross-product benchmark.  Each scenario tests a distinct
spatial relationship between the tumour and the beam, ranging from perfect alignment
(diagonal) to complete misalignment (off-diagonal) and the pathological two-peak case
in which the tumour centre of mass lies between the two beam lobes.

\subsection{Training the MLP Corrector}

The training library consists of $N_{\mathrm{train}} = 200$ PDE simulations with
randomised spatial configurations.  For each simulation, the tumour IC centre and
beam centre are sampled independently and uniformly from $[0.15L, 0.85L]^2$,
excluding a small neighbourhood of the sixteen named test-scenario centres to
prevent data leakage.  Seventy per cent of simulations use single-peak IC and beam
profiles; the remaining thirty per cent use two-peak profiles (separated by 3.5 to 6
spatial units in a random direction).

Each simulation produces $T = 801$ time steps, yielding
$200 \times 801 = 160\,200$ (feature, residual) training pairs.
The MLP is trained for $200$ epochs with the Adam optimiser \cite{kingma2015adam}
(learning rate $10^{-3}$, cosine decay \cite{loshchilov2017sgdr}) and batch size
$1024$.  The implementation uses PyTorch \cite{paszke2019pytorch} and SciPy
\cite{virtanen2020scipy}.  The final training MSE is approximately
$2 \times 10^{-7}$, corresponding to a mean absolute residual below $0.5\%$ of the
normalised mass scale.  The complete offline training pipeline runs in under
two minutes on a standard CPU.

Figure~\ref{fig:4x4} shows the normalised tumour mass $U(t) = y(t)/L^2$ for all
sixteen spatial scenarios.  Each panel compares the PDE ground truth (solid black),
the plain ODE surrogate (dash-dot grey), and the ODE+MLP corrector (dashed orange).

\begin{figure}[htb]

\caption{Normalised tumour mass trajectories for $4 \times 4 = 16$ spatial scenarios.

The ODE+MLP corrector closely tracks the PDE ground truth across all scenarios,
including spatially misaligned cases where the plain ODE exhibits systematic error.}
\label{fig:4x4}

The plain ODE consistently overestimates tumour mass for beam-aligned scenarios
(columns matching the IC row) and underestimates the radiation effect for misaligned
scenarios, reflecting the second error source in~\eqref{eq:residual}.  The ODE+MLP
corrector eliminates both biases by conditioning on the misalignment distance $d/L$
and the effective beam amplitude $\bar{R}$.

\subsection{Computational Efficiency}

Figure~\ref{fig:timing} shows wall-clock time as a function of the PDE grid size $N$
for all surrogates.

\begin{figure}[htb]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_timing.png}
\caption{Left: absolute wall-clock time vs.\ grid size $N$ (logarithmic scale).
Right: speedup relative to PDE.  The ODE and ODE+MLP are effectively grid-independent;
the MLP forward pass over the full trajectory adds less than $1$\,ms.}
\label{fig:timing}
\end{figure}

For the default grid ($N = 50$) the PDE requires approximately $28$\,ms per
simulation.  The ODE surrogate takes less than $0.5$\,ms; the ODE+MLP adds a single
forward pass over the 801-step trajectory, bringing the total to approximately
$1$\,ms, achieving a \textbf{28× speedup} over the PDE.  At the largest tested grid
($N = 100$, corresponding to four times the degrees of freedom) the PDE requires
$87$\,ms while the ODE+MLP remains below $2$\,ms, a \textbf{speedup exceeding 50×}.  The MLP forward pass is constant in $N$ because the ten-dimensional feature
vector is computed from scalar statistics, not from grid operations.
